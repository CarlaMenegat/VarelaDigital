Data Model

The Varela Digital Knowledge Graph stores the project’s data in RDF (serialised as JSON-LD), and is populated with reused and project-specific classes and properties. The documentation below presents the core modules of the model and the main modelling choices adopted to represent the collection, its documentary units, and the semantic relations extracted from the texts.

Module 1 — The Collection

The collection model (M1) describes the Varela Collection as an intellectual and editorial entity. The Varela Collection is modelled as an instance of fabio:WorkCollection, representing a coherent scholarly aggregation of documentary works united by provenance, historical context, and editorial mediation.

Each individual document in the collection is modelled as an instance of frbr:Work and linked to the collection through the frbr:part relation. This modelling choice allows heterogeneous documentary genres — including letters, official documents, accounts, and legal texts — to coexist within a unified conceptual framework, while remaining distinguishable at the level of documentary typology.

The collection model provides the structural entry point for the Knowledge Graph, enabling navigation, aggregation, and querying across documents and supporting the integration of multiple textual expressions, material manifestations, and editorial realizations. By adopting a FRBR-aligned approach through the FaBiO ontology, the model treats the collection as a first-class intellectual object, independent of any single physical or digital instantiation.

Module 2 — Letters and Documentary Models

The second module (M2) describes letters as documentary works and models their different material and editorial manifestations within the Varela Digital corpus. Each letter is represented as an instance of fabio:Letter, understood as a subclass of frbr:Work, and linked to the collection through frbr:part.

At the level of the letter as a work, the model records the document identifier (dcterms:identifier), the sending date (dcterms:date), the place of dispatch (dcterms:spatial), the sender (dcterms:creator), the addressee (pro:addressee), and the internal textual structure through discrete textual segments (doco:contains, doco:TextChunk).

The model supports multiple manifestations of the same letter through frbr:embodiment. Manuscript letters are represented as fabio:AnalogManifestation and linked to physical exemplars (frbr:Item) with identifiers and physical extent. Printed letters, as published in the Anais do Arquivo Histórico do Rio Grande do Sul, are modelled as analog manifestations with publication date (dcterms:issued), publisher (dcterms:publisher), rights statements, and an explicit editorial interpretation process (hico:InterpretationAct, hico:interpretedBy). Bibliographic information such as volume and page range is associated with the corresponding item. Digital letters are represented as fabio:DigitalManifestation, linked to digital items carrying identifiers and rights information.

This module provides a unified framework for representing the intellectual identity of letters and their diverse material and editorial forms, while maintaining compatibility with FRBR-based bibliographic modelling and Linked Open Data practices.

Module 3 — HRAO Relations Extracted from Text

Semantic relations model (HRAO)
The HRAO module of the Varela Digital data model represents the semantic relations extracted from the letters and recorded through standoff annotation. Rather than introducing new classes, HRAO acts as a controlled ontology of domain-specific owl:ObjectProperty predicates that connect existing nodes in the Knowledge Graph—primarily foaf:Person, foaf:Agent, san:Event, and geo:SpatialThing. This design keeps the model interoperable and FRBR-aligned while making historically meaningful relations explicit and queryable.

The model captures three main relation families. First, interpersonal relations link persons to persons through properties such as foaf:knows and HRAO predicates (e.g., hrao:hasKinship, hrao:servedUnder, hrao:followedPoliticalAlignmentOf), reflecting social ties and political positioning expressed in the corpus. Second, institutional relations connect persons to organizations using, for example, schema:memberOf and organization-to-organization hierarchies via schema:subOrganizationOf; these links support the reconstruction of affiliation networks and administrative structures. Third, event-based relations connect persons to san:Event via hrao:participatedInEvent, while events are anchored spatially through san:hasPlace to geo:SpatialThing, enabling temporal-spatial analyses of participation and movement across the correspondence.
